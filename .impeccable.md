## Design Context

### Users
Non-engineer and junior builders who shipped something fast with Lovable, Bolt, Cursor, or ChatGPT. They're proud of what they built and a little anxious about what they might have missed. They use this product on a laptop, usually in a browser, mid-afternoon. They want to feel like a professional caught their back — not like they're being scolded by a compliance tool.

### Brand Personality
Warm, calm, literate. "We caught what got missed." The tone is a knowledgeable friend, never alarmist. Three words: **careful · reassuring · precise**.

### Aesthetic Direction
Refined editorial minimal. Think a high-quality independent tech magazine: warm off-white paper (`#f7f5f2`), Instrument Serif for display and numerals (feels like type set by hand), DM Sans weight 300 for body (precise and understated). One sharp accent: `#c0392b` (a muted crimson) for risk and emphasis — used sparingly so it always signals something real. Borders are exactly 1px `#e2deda` ink rules — never heavier, never a box-shadow parade.

Light mode only. The product is used in the daytime; the warm off-white feels deliberate, not a retreat.

Anti-references: anything that looks like a SaaS security dashboard from 2022 — dark backgrounds with neon green, badge-heavy UIs, "scan complete" confetti, aggressive red everywhere.

### Design Principles
1. **Whitespace is the loudest thing.** Crowd nothing. Let elements breathe. Generous vertical rhythm over density.
2. **Serif numerals as artifact.** Finding counts, severity numbers, and stats always render in Instrument Serif — they feel like a typeset report, not a dashboard widget.
3. **Motion serves narrative.** Every animation should have a reason: something appears, a process completes, a state changes. No decorative looping. No ambient particle fields.
4. **Rhythm over repetition.** Vary spacing for hierarchy. Not every card has the same padding. Not every element has the same weight. Asymmetry feels more designed than grids.
5. **Red means something.** The `#c0392b` accent is reserved for actual risk — critical findings, errors, the italic "Is it secure?" hook. Overuse kills it.
